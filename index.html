The Byzantine Generals Problem

Multiple generals have surrounded Byzantium with their armies and must attack simultaneously, but their means of communicating with one another about an attack strategy, sending messages to one another using their messengers, could be imitated by the besieged Byzantines. Additionally, some generals have concerns that there are traitorous generals amoung them who will attempt to side with the Byzantines, and they'll purposely try to sabotage the timing of other generals' attacks with their messages.

When incoming messengers arrive to claim it is some general's intention to attack at some time, recipient generals need a way to be certain whether messages they recieve are brought from messengers of allies, or decoys sent by Byzantines or traitors. No stated time to strike can be trusted without some solution to this problem.

Because just one general sending his army to attack at the incorrect time could drive the entire system to catastrophic failure, even one fault in a potential solution that drives any loyal general to act with the belief that a false message is true for any amount of time, so that he may be fooled and convinced into attacking without all loyal generals having previously agreed on that time to attack, when certain that all other loyal generals would be so coordinated, Byzantium will have the ability to defeat them in battle if they don't develop a full proof system to subvert all unreliable sources of information.


The allegory was developed to describe a situation in which, in order to avoid catastrophic failure of the system, the system's actors must agree on a concerted strategy, but some of these actors are unreliable.

This problem does not change when internet is brought into the equation. Tools for encrypted information transmissions across far distances instantaneously can only complement the solution to their problem. Encryption is encrypted to be decrypted by people. Electrical signals transforming digital databases can be imitated and life and death are at stake for one single failure.
