The Byzantine Generals Problem

Multiple generals have surrounded Byzantium with their armies and must attack simultaneously, but their means of communicating with one another about an attack strategy, sending messages to one another using their messengers, could be imitated by the besieged Byzantines. Additionally, some generals have concerns that there are traitorous generals amoung them who will attempt to side with the Byzantines, and they'll purposely try to sabotage the timing of other generals' attacks with their messages.

When incoming messengers arrive to claim it is some general's intention to attack at some time, recipient generals need a way to be certain whether messages they recieve are brought from messengers of allies, or decoys sent by Byzantines or traitors. No stated time to strike can be trusted without some solution to this problem.

Because just one general sending his army to attack at the incorrect time could drive the entire system to catastrophic failure, even one fault in a potential solution that drives any loyal general to act with the belief that a false message is true for any amount of time, so that he may be fooled and convinced into attacking without all loyal generals having previously agreed on that time to attack when certain that all other loyal generals would be so coordinated, Byzantium will defeat them in battle without development of a full proof system to subvert unreliable sources of information.


The allegory was developed to describe a situation in which, in order to avoid catastrophic failure of the system, the system's actors must agree on a concerted strategy, but some of these actors are unreliable.

A centralized system may be possible for the generals to adopt using known and mutually trusted messengers to handle all of their communications, but the allegory specifically calls for messengers to be able to be imitated by besieged Byzantines. Also, any faults cannot be reliably reversed in afterthought to restore broken faith in the network. A single fraudulent message believed means potentially instant failure if a general believes and acts upon a message from a decoy which falsely declares the time to strike is immediately.
